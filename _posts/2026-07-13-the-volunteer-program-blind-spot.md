## Why Vague Volunteer Roles Undermine Engagement

Volunteers aren't mind readers. If you're not defining the role clearly, you're unintentionally setting them (and your organization) up for confusion, frustration, and disengagement.

A well-written volunteer role should answer the following:

### What is the purpose of this role?

- Why does this role exist, and how does it contribute to the organization's overall mission and goals?

### What tasks will they be responsible for?

- What specific duties will the volunteer perform?
- Are these tasks routine or event-based?
- How will these tasks impact the community or the program?

### What skills or traits are ideal?

- What qualifications, experience, or attributes will make a volunteer successful in this role?
- Are specific technical skills or soft skills required?
- Should the volunteer have previous experience or training?

### Who do they report to?

- What is the chain of command for this role?
- Who is the main point of contact for questions, support, and feedback?
- Will they be working independently or as part of a team?

### What's the time commitment?

- How many hours per week or month are expected?
- Are there specific timeframes or deadlines (e.g., ongoing support, seasonal events, or one-time projects)?
- Are there any flexible scheduling options or required shifts?

### What are the expected outcomes or goals for this role?

- What will success look like in this role?
- Are there measurable objectives (e.g., specific number of events, volunteer hours, etc.)?
- How will progress be tracked?

### What kind of support or resources will be provided?

- Will there be training, mentorship, or shadowing?
- Are there materials or tools needed to complete tasks (e.g., software, uniforms, equipment)?
- How will volunteers receive ongoing guidance or supervision?

### How will this role contribute to volunteer engagement and retention?

- What makes this role meaningful for volunteers?
- How will volunteers feel a sense of accomplishment or contribution?
- Is there room for growth or more involvement?

### What safety or risk considerations need to be addressed?

- Are there safety protocols or regulations that volunteers must follow?
- Is there any training related to safety or liability specific to this role?

### What is the volunteer's relationship with other team members or staff?

- Will they work alongside staff, other volunteers, or clients?
- How should they interact with others, and what are the expectations for collaboration or communication?

### Are there any legal or compliance issues to consider for this role?

- Does the role require background checks, certifications, or adherence to specific laws (e.g., privacy laws, safety standards)?
- Are there age or eligibility restrictions?

### What benefits or incentives are available for this role?

- Are there any perks, recognition programs, or networking opportunities?
- Will volunteers receive letters of recommendation, certificates, or other professional development support?

Without clarity, volunteers may underperform or feel like they're wasting time.

> **Pro tip:** Don't treat volunteer roles as "filler." Design them with intention—just like a job description.
{: .post-callout}

> **Pro tip 2:** Use catchy, exciting tones for your role descriptions making the role sound appealing and inviting for potential volunteers.
>
> Example: "Ready to make a real impact and have some fun while doing it? This role is about being part of a passionate, dynamic team where your skills shine, and your contributions truly matter. You'll gain experience, meet amazing people, and have a blast while helping us move the mission forward!
